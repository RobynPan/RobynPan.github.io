# Making a Smart Pocket-watch

tags: Hardware, Wearable, Moto360, Android Wear, Hacking, Berlin, Flea Market

I don't like using computers. I also don't like sitting at laptops. They cause me to warp my posture around a device that is cludgy and restricts my movements. So when I heard about wearables, I was pretty psyched. I want a digital experience that isn't cumbersome, that is molded to how I choose to live my life, rather than having to stick to the given infrastructure.

I was pretty unimpressed by the first smartwatches that came out. They seemed like the typical chic gadgets people spend too much on and then never use. I have never felt the need to know how many steps I've been or how fast my heart rate is.

When I recently got my hands on an old first Gen Moto 360, I certainly wasn't going to turn down the chance to try it out. I went online and found a tutorial on how to install the terminal app [Termux](https://termux.com/) on it. I was inspired. I could run around with a terminal on my wrist. I imagined using SSH to access any of my systems remotely from my watch.

## The Find

Skip forward to last summer, when I was browsing a flea-market in Charlottenburg, Berlin, and came across a bunch of old pocket-watches. I wanted one. I had no use for one, but I had a mighty need. That's when inspiration struck. The beauty of a pocket-watch terminal was too good to miss. I started searching for a pocket-watch that would match the size of the Moto 360. A week or two later: success!

## The Build

The teardown of the Moto 360 was the point of no return. Even after heating the watch, I still couldn't lever the back off without breaking it. With the back in pieces, I was never going to wear it as a watch again. I clipped off most of the black plastic housing around the chip until it was just slim enough to slip into the hollow pocket-watch casing.

I removed the glass face of the pocket-watch when I realised it would obstruct both the touch-screen and the housing closing properly. With the glass removed, the screen slipped perfectly into the empty frame.

The crown pin was a challenge — in the original Huber pocket-watch it turned a series of cogs, going deep into the mechanism. I used an angle-grinder to shorten it, then bent a piece of metal into shape so the pin would reliably press the Moto 360's side button. A few tests: worked like a charm. Hot glue, clamp, done.

*(You might notice a marred part of the frame surrounding the screen — that's where I used a blowtorch to heat it for the hot glue. Still need to find a method of pickling it without hurting the electronics.)*
